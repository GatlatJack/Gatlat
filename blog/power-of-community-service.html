The Power of Community Service

How volunteering at Resilience Montreal has shaped my perspective on giving back to the community
Community Volunteering Personal

When I first arrived in Montreal as an international student, I was focused entirely on academics, adjusting to a new culture, and building my career. The idea of volunteering seemed like a luxury I couldn't afford with my packed schedule. Little did I know that my decision to volunteer at Resilience Montreal would become one of the most transformative experiences of my time in Canada.

Finding Resilience Montreal

I discovered Resilience Montreal during a campus volunteer fair in my second month at McGill. The organization's mission resonated with me immediately: supporting newcomers and vulnerable community members through various programs and services. Having been a newcomer myself, I understood the challenges of navigating a new city, culture, and system.

"The best way to find yourself is to lose yourself in the service of others." - Mahatma Gandhi

This quote, displayed prominently in Resilience Montreal's office, encapsulates exactly what I experienced through my volunteer journey.

My Role and Responsibilities

I started as a general volunteer, helping with various tasks around the organization. Over time, my responsibilities expanded to include:

  • Language Support: Helping newcomers with English and French language practice
  • Technology Assistance: Teaching basic computer skills and helping with online services
  • Community Events: Organizing and participating in cultural integration events
  • Administrative Support: Helping with documentation and client intake processes
  • Peer Mentorship: Supporting other international students in their adjustment process

The People I Met

Volunteering introduced me to an incredibly diverse group of people, each with unique stories and experiences:

Sarah - A Syrian Refugee Family's Matriarch

Sarah arrived in Montreal with her three children, speaking no English or French. Over months of weekly sessions, I watched her transform from someone overwhelmed by simple tasks like grocery shopping to a confident woman who started her own small catering business. Her resilience taught me that determination can overcome almost any obstacle.

Ahmed - The Eager Student

A teenage refugee who had missed three years of formal education, Ahmed was determined to catch up academically. I helped him with mathematics and computer skills. Seeing his excitement when he finally mastered creating his first PowerPoint presentation reminded me never to take my educational opportunities for granted.

Maria - The Professional Starting Over

Maria was a trained architect in her home country but needed to navigate Canada's professional certification process. I helped her with document preparation and online applications. Her perseverance in rebuilding her career from scratch inspired me to approach my own challenges with greater patience.

Lessons Learned Through Service

1. Perspective is Everything

My academic stress about assignments and exams felt different after meeting people who were learning to read for the first time as adults. While my challenges remained valid, I gained perspective on what truly constitutes a significant obstacle versus a temporary inconvenience.

2. Small Actions, Big Impact

Teaching someone how to use email or helping them fill out a government form might seem trivial, but these small acts had profound impacts on people's lives. I learned that you don't need to change the world in grand gestures; consistent small actions can transform individual lives.

3. The Universal Nature of Human Struggle and Joy

Despite language barriers and cultural differences, I discovered that human emotions and experiences are remarkably universal. The pride in a child's first successful school presentation, the relief of finally understanding a government letter, or the joy of making a new friend transcend all boundaries.

4. Receiving More Than Giving

While I went in thinking I was helping others, I quickly realized I was receiving far more than I was giving. The clients taught me about resilience, gratitude, and finding joy in simple victories. They shared their cultures, foods, and perspectives, enriching my own worldview immeasurably.

Personal Growth Through Service

Enhanced Communication Skills

Working with people who spoke limited English improved my ability to communicate clearly and patiently. I learned to break down complex concepts into simple steps and to use non-verbal communication effectively. These skills proved invaluable in my academic presentations and group projects.

Cultural Competency

Interacting with people from dozens of different countries and cultures expanded my understanding of global perspectives. This cultural awareness enhanced my ability to work in diverse teams and appreciate different approaches to problem-solving.

Leadership Development

As I became more experienced, I was asked to train new volunteers and coordinate small events. These leadership opportunities built my confidence and project management skills in a low-pressure, supportive environment.

Time Management

Balancing volunteering with my academic responsibilities forced me to become more efficient with my time. I learned to prioritize tasks effectively and discovered that having a commitment outside of academics actually made me more productive, not less.

Connecting Technology and Service

My background in computer science allowed me to contribute in unique ways:

Digital Literacy Programs

I developed and led workshops on basic computer skills, internet safety, and using online government services. Teaching these skills to adults who had never used a computer was both challenging and incredibly rewarding.

Website Improvements

I volunteered to help update Resilience Montreal's website, making it more user-friendly and accessible for people with varying levels of digital literacy. This project allowed me to apply my technical skills in a meaningful way while gaining real-world web development experience.

Virtual Support During COVID-19

When the pandemic moved services online, I helped transition some of our support programs to virtual formats. This experience taught me about the importance of accessible technology design and the digital divide's impact on vulnerable communities.

Challenges and Growth Opportunities

Language Barriers

Initially, communicating with clients who spoke little English was frustrating for both parties. However, this challenge pushed me to develop creativity in communication and patience in understanding. I learned basic phrases in several languages and became skilled at using translation apps effectively.

Emotional Intensity

Hearing stories of trauma and hardship was emotionally challenging. I had to learn to provide support without becoming overwhelmed by others' pain. The organization provided excellent training on maintaining professional boundaries while still showing empathy and care.

Time Commitment Pressures

There were periods when academic demands conflicted with volunteer commitments. I learned to communicate honestly with the organization about my availability and to maintain consistent but realistic commitments.

The Ripple Effect

My volunteer work created positive ripple effects I never anticipated:

Inspiring Others

Several of my classmates joined volunteer programs after hearing about my experiences. Some even started volunteering with Resilience Montreal themselves, expanding the organization's capacity to help more people.

Career Connections

Through volunteering, I met professionals from various fields who became valuable mentors and network connections. Several offered insights into career paths I hadn't previously considered.

Scholarship and Recognition

My volunteer work was recognized by the university and contributed to scholarship applications. More importantly, it provided rich material for personal statements and interviews, demonstrating my commitment to community engagement.

Memorable Moments

The Graduation Celebration

When Ahmed graduated from high school after years of struggle, Resilience Montreal organized a small celebration. Seeing his beaming face as he showed off his diploma reminded me why education is such a powerful tool for transformation. His mother's tears of joy were a reminder of how individual achievements impact entire families.

Sarah's Business Launch

The day Sarah launched her catering business, she insisted I be her first customer. The pride in her voice as she took my order in fluent English was incredible. Later, she told me that our practice conversations had given her the confidence to interact with customers.

The Multicultural Potluck

During a community event, clients from over 20 countries brought traditional foods to share. Sitting in that room, listening to people share stories in multiple languages while children played together, I felt deeply connected to the beautiful diversity of Montreal's immigrant community.

Lessons for Fellow Students

For other students considering volunteer work, I offer these insights:

Start Small but Stay Consistent

You don't need to commit 20 hours per week. Even 2-3 hours weekly, maintained consistently, can make a significant impact and provide meaningful experiences.

Choose Organizations Aligned with Your Values

Volunteer work should resonate with your personal values and interests. When the mission matters to you, the work feels meaningful rather than burdensome.

Be Open to Unexpected Learning

I expected to teach and help others, but I didn't anticipate how much I would learn about myself, develop new skills, or gain fresh perspectives on my own challenges.

Use Your Unique Skills

Every student has something valuable to contribute. Whether it's language skills, technical abilities, artistic talents, or simply enthusiasm, there's a way to apply your strengths in service to others.

The Long-term Impact

My volunteer experience has permanently shaped my career goals and personal values:

Career Direction

Working with Resilience Montreal reinforced my interest in developing technology solutions for social good. I'm now exploring career paths that combine my technical skills with community impact, such as working for non-profits or developing applications that serve underrepresented communities.

Personal Values

The experience cemented my belief in the importance of giving back to community. I've committed to maintaining volunteer involvement throughout my career, regardless of how busy life becomes.

Global Perspective

Understanding the challenges faced by refugees and newcomers has made me more aware of global issues and my role as a global citizen. This perspective influences my academic choices and future goals.

Continuing the Journey

Although my formal volunteer role with Resilience Montreal has evolved as my academic schedule changed, my commitment to community service continues:

  • I mentor new international students at McGill
  • I participate in community tech literacy events
  • I support fundraising efforts for local non-profits
  • I advocate for immigrant rights and support services

A Call to Action

To my fellow students and young professionals reading this: I challenge you to find a way to give back to your community. The specific cause or organization doesn't matter as much as the commitment to service.

In our increasingly connected yet divided world, community service provides opportunities to build bridges, develop empathy, and create positive change. It reminds us that despite our differences, we share common hopes, dreams, and challenges.

Gratitude and Moving Forward

I am deeply grateful to Resilience Montreal for providing me with the opportunity to serve, learn, and grow. The organization's staff, volunteers, and clients taught me invaluable lessons about resilience, community, and the power of human connection.

Most importantly, they showed me that being part of a community means more than just living in the same placeâ€”it means actively contributing to the wellbeing of others and recognizing that our individual success is interconnected with the success of our neighbors.

Final Thoughts

Community service is not just about helping others; it's about recognizing our shared humanity and building the kind of world we want to live in. In a time when technology often isolates us, volunteer work provides authentic human connection and reminds us of our capacity for compassion.

As I continue my academic and professional journey, the lessons learned through community service remain central to who I am and who I aspire to become. The power of community service lies not just in the problems it solves or the people it helps, but in its ability to transform those who serve.

To anyone considering volunteer work: take that first step. Your community needs you, and you might be surprised by how much you need your community in return.

Gatlat Deng Bum

Computer Science Student at McGill University
